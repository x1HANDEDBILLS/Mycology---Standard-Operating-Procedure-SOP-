# 24 - Long-Term Preservation

Live mycelium will eventually eat all the nutrients in its container and die. To preserve your isolated genetics, you must stall their growth using cold temperatures (the refrigerator). 

**Warning:** Never freeze live mycelium. The ice crystals will rupture the cells and kill the culture. The ideal storage temperature is **38°F to 40°F**.

## 1. Storing Agar Plates (3-6 Months)
1.  Ensure the agar plate is fully colonized and 100% free of contamination.
2.  Wrap the outside edge of the plate tightly with Parafilm or grafting tape to seal out air.
3.  Place the wrapped plates into a clean ziplock bag and store them in the refrigerator.

## 2. Storing Liquid Culture Syringes (6-12 Months)
1.  Inside the SAB, draw your liquid culture into sterile luer-lock syringes.
2.  Remove the needle and twist on a sterile luer-lock cap to prevent leaks.
3.  Place the capped syringes into a ziplock bag and store in the refrigerator. 
4.  *Note:* The mycelium will clump together in the cold. Shake the syringe vigorously before using it again.

## 3. Storing LC in Self-Healing Vials (1+ Years)
*Sterile vacuum vials are the safest way to store LC because you never have to open a lid to access the culture.*
1.  Purchase sterile, sealed 10ml or 20ml self-healing glass vials.
2.  Inside the SAB, wipe the vial's rubber stopper with alcohol.
3.  Inject your freshly made liquid culture directly into the sealed vial. 
4.  Store the vial in the refrigerator. Whenever you need culture, wipe the top, pierce it with a sterile syringe, and draw out exactly what you need without exposing the rest of the fluid to open air.

## 4. Agar Master Slants (2-5 Years)
*Slants are the ultimate long-term storage. Because the agar is poured into a test tube at an angle (a slant), the mycelium has deep nutrients to slowly consume over several years in the fridge.*
1.  Prepare your standard agar recipe, but include a small piece of wood (like a piece of a popsicle stick) inside the test tube before pressure cooking. The wood gives the mycelium a complex carbon source to slowly digest.
2.  Pressure cook the test tubes, then let them cool while leaning at a 45-degree angle.
3.  Inside the SAB, drop a tiny piece of cloned tissue or clean agar onto the bottom of the slant.
4.  Let it colonize at room temperature for 2 weeks.
5.  Once the mycelium has grown over the wood, cap the tube tightly, wrap it in Parafilm, and store it in the back of the refrigerator.
